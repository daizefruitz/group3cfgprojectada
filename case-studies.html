Manal Al Sharif

Born: 25 April 1979 (age 44 years)
Manal Al Sharif is a women's rights activist from Saudi Arabia who gained international attention in 2011 for her activism in the "Women2Drive" campaign. She played a significant role in challenging the laws that restrict women from driving in the country.

Manal Al Sharif was born in 1979 in Saudi Arabia. She grew up in the city of Mecca and later studied computer science at the King Abdulaziz University in Jeddah. After completing her studies, she worked as a computer security specialist at Saudi Aramco, the state-owned oil company. In 2011, Al Sharif became involved in a campaign called "Women2Drive," which aimed to challenge the laws that prohibit women from driving in Saudi Arabia. She filmed herself driving a car and uploaded the video on social media, which went viral and sparked national and international attention.

As a women's rights activist, Al Sharif has been a vocal critic of the Saudi government's policies on gender equality, particularly the laws that restrict women's freedom of movement. She has also called for an end to the male guardianship system, a practice in which women must obtain the permission of a male relative to travel, work, marry, or even seek healthcare. Al Sharif was arrested in May 2011 for defying the ban on driving and was held in prison for ten days. Following her release, she continued her activism and was later awarded the Vaclav Havel Prize for Creative Dissent in 2012 by the Human Rights Foundation. In 2013, she published a memoir titled "Daring to Drive: A Saudi Woman's Awakening," which chronicled her personal journey as a women's rights activist.

Al Sharif's activism has had a significant impact on raising awareness about women's rights in Saudi Arabia. Her actions have inspired many women to challenge the status quo and demand greater gender equality in the country. In 2018, the Saudi government lifted the ban on women's driving, a move that was largely attributed to the efforts of the "Women2Drive" campaign. Overall, Manal Al Sharif's story serves as a powerful example of the impact that individuals can have in bringing about positive change in their communities and driving social progress.

Chantelle Bell

Born: 1994 (age 29 years)
Chantell Bell is a female entrepreneur and co-founder of Syrona Women, a startup that develops innovative medical devices for women's health. Bell, along with co-founder Anya Roy, developed a simple and cost-effective cervical cancer screening device that can be used at home. The device has the potential to revolutionize cervical cancer screening and reduce the number of deaths from this deadly disease.

Bell was born in London, UK, and raised in Jamaica. Her parents moved to the UK when she was a child, and she grew up in East London. She attended the University of Cambridge, where she studied Bioscience and met her co-founder, Anya Roy.

Bell and Roy were both passionate about women's health and wanted to make a difference in this field. After researching the statistics on cervical cancer, they realized that there was a great need for better screening options, particularly in developing countries where access to healthcare is limited. They decided to develop a simple and affordable screening device that women could use at home. After several years of research and development, they launched Syrona Women in 2018. The startup has since won several awards and has been featured in numerous media outlets. Since launching Syrona Women, Bell and Roy have received numerous accolades and awards. They were named AccelerateHER Scotland's "One to Watch" in 2019 and won the Tata InnoVista Health Award in 2020. They were also selected to participate in the prestigious Bethnal Green Ventures accelerator program. In addition to these awards, Bell and Roy have been invited to speak at several conferences and events. They have presented their work at Cambridge Wireless UK and the MEDICA conference in Dusseldorf, Germany.

Syrona Women has raised hundreds of thousands of dollars to date, and Bell and Roy have ambitious plans for the future. They plan to expand their product line and develop new screening devices for other women's health issues. They also hope to partner with healthcare providers and governments to make their products more widely available.Chantell Bell's work with Syrona Women is a shining example of how entrepreneurship can be used to tackle important social issues. By developing a simple and affordable cervical cancer screening device, Bell and Roy have the potential to save countless lives. Their dedication to women's health is an inspiration to entrepreneurs everywhere.

Annie Easley

Born: 23 April 1933 - 25 June 2011
Annie Easley was an African-American mathematician, computer scientist, and rocket scientist who made significant contributions to the field of energy conversion systems and alternative energy technology. She was one of the first computer programmers at NASA, where she worked for over three decades.

Annie Easley was born in Birmingham, Alabama, on April 23, 1933. She grew up in the segregated South and attended a segregated school. Despite the odds, Easley excelled in school and earned a scholarship to Xavier University in New Orleans, Louisiana, where she earned a degree in pharmacy in 1954. After working as a pharmacist for a few years, Easley went back to school and earned her degree in mathematics from Cleveland State University in Ohio in 1977. She also took courses in computer science and began her career as a computer programmer.

In 1955, Easley started working at the National Advisory Committee for Aeronautics (NACA), which later became NASA. Easley joined NASA before the start of the Space Age, and she witnessed the transformation of NASA from its early days to its modern era. During her career, Easley worked on various projects, including the Centaur rocket, which was a critical component of the Voyager, Viking, and Pioneer space probes. She also worked on the creation of software for energy conversion systems, and her work led to the development of new technologies for the production of clean energy. Easley was one of the few African American women working in the industry during the 1960s, and her contributions to the field helped pave the way for other women and minorities pursuing careers in science and technology.

Annie Easley was a true trailblazer in the field of science and technology. She persevered through adversity and became a prominent figure in the industry, known for her work on alternative energy technology and her contributions to NASA. She passed away in 2011, but her legacy lives on as an inspiration to future generations of scientists and technologists. In 2019, NASA honored Easley by naming the street in front of its Cleveland facility "Annie Easley Drive." Easley is also featured in the Women of NASA LEGO set, which celebrates the many women who have made significant contributions to NASA and space exploration.

Ada Lovelace

Born: 10 December 1815 - 27 November 1852
Ada Lovelace, born in London in 1815, was the only legitimate child of Lord Byron, the famous poet. Her mother, Lady Byron, separated from Lord Byron just a month after Ada's birth, and never allowed her daughter to meet him. Instead, Lady Byron stressed the importance of mathematics and science to Ada and hired tutors to teach her advanced mathematics, which was uncommon for a girl in the 19th century. Ada showed great aptitude for mathematics at a young age and went on to become a mathematician and writer.

Key Challenges:

  1. Overcoming Gender Barriers: In the 19th century, women were not encouraged to pursue careers in science, technology, engineering, and mathematics (STEM). Ada faced numerous challenges due to societal norms that viewed women as inferior to men in intellectual fields. It wasn't until many years later that Ada's work was recognized and she became known as "the world's first computer programmer."
  2. Understanding Charles Babbage's Analytical Engine: Ada had a deep understanding of mathematics and became fascinated with Charles Babbage's "analytical engine," which was a forerunner to modern computers. Babbage had invented the machine, but it was Ada who recognized its potential and envisioned its capabilities beyond basic calculation. She saw that it could be programmed to produce more than just numerical tables.
  3. Communicating Her Ideas: Ada's ideas were groundbreaking, but she had to find a way to communicate them in a way that others could understand. She wrote a detailed set of notes on the analytical engine, which included what is now recognized as the first computer program. Her notes were written in English, but the machine was designed to work with punch cards that contained binary code. Ada had to translate her ideas into a format that the machine could understand.

Ada Lovelace's work on the analytical engine was revolutionary and laid the groundwork for the development of modern computing. She identified the potential of the machine beyond simple calculations and recognized its potential as a tool for creativity. Ada's notes, including what is now recognized as the world's first computer program, were written about a century before the first computer was built. Ada's innovative thinking and persistent pursuit of knowledge continues to inspire women in the STEM fields to this day.

In a time when women were not encouraged to pursue careers in science and technology, Ada Lovelace stood out as a visionary computer programmer. Her work on Charles Babbage's analytical engine paved the way for modern computing and highlighted the creative potential of machines. Even though her notes were written over a century before the first computer was built, Ada's ideas were so forward-thinking that they are still relevant today. Ada Lovelace's legacy serves as an inspiration for women in STEM and highlights the importance of education, persistence, and creativity in overcoming barriers.

Emmanuelle Marie Charpentier

Born: 11 December 1968 (age 54 years)

She is widely known for her work in the field of CRISPR-Cas9 gene editing, which has revolutionized the field of molecular biology. In 2020, she was awarded the Nobel Prize in Chemistry, along with Jennifer Doudna, for their development of this ground-breaking technology.

She then moved to the Pasteur Institute in Paris, where she completed her PhD and postdoctoral research under the supervision of Philippe Sansonetti, a renowned microbiologist.

Charpentier began her independent research career at the Max Planck Institute for Infection Biology in Berlin, Germany. In collaboration with Jennifer Doudna, she discovered the CRISPR-Cas9 system in bacteria, a revolutionary approach to genetic engineering.

The CRISPR-Cas9 system allows scientists to edit the genome of living organisms with unprecedented precision. The technology makes it possible to cut and paste DNA sequences with incredible ease, opening up new avenues for biomedical research and personalized medicine. Charpentier's work has largely involved the study of CRISPR-Cas9 in Streptococcus pyogenes bacteria. She demonstrated that the system could be harnessed to cut DNA at specific locations, allowing precise gene editing.

Charpentier's work has had a profound impact on biology, medicine, and biotechnology.
